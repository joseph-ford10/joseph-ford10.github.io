Neuroplasticity and the growth mindset

Learning can be hard. How can you stay motivated when it feels like you'll never get it?

4 February 2023

Neuroplasticity

In the broadest sense, the term neuroplasticity refers to the brain’s capacity to change. Although neuroplasticity in children has long been accepted, until comparatively recently it was thought that adult brains were fixed after a certain point (Fuchs & Flügge, 2014). While it might be true that brains are less malleable post-adolescence (Kolb & Gibb, 2011) and that childhood and adult neuroplasticity are different (Lillard & Erisir, 2014), recent research has shown that adult brains are not as fixed as was once thought (Lillard & Erisir, 2014).

A famous and striking illustration of this comes from a study focused on black cab drivers, who are required to memorise and navigate thousands of streets. This study suggests that these drivers have differences in brain structure compared to a bus drivers, who, while also spending a lot of time driving around London, follow fixed routes (Maguire et al., 2006).

Although black cab drivers’ knowledge represents a particularly intensive and extensive form of learning, as Boaler (undated) notes, this research has implications for learners of other types (see Lillard and Erisir (2011) for an overview of other studies of learning and neuroplasticity in adults). It is easy, past a certain point, to think of yourself as being wired in a certain way, impervious to new information or ways of doing things. This way of thinking can have a negative impact when you’re struggling with a difficult topic. Instead of attributing the difficulty to the complexity of the topic itself, you can see yourself as ‘unteachable’. In the worst case scenario, you might even give up.

Neuroplasticity shows us that we retain our capacity to learn (and relearn—see work on neuroplasticity and stroke recovery (Su & Xu, 2020)), which, for someone learning something new (how to code, say!), can be a useful thing to bear in mind. This is where having a growth mindset becomes relevant.

Growth mindset

If neuroplasticity suggests a capacity for learning, then having a growth mindset means being aware of and believing in that capacity. Carol Dweck, a key figure in mindset research, contrasts the growth mindset with the ‘fixed’ mindset, where learners believe that they have a finite capacity that they cannot go beyond (Dweck, 2013).

Although there have been debates and controversies in the research and application of mindsets (see Yeager & Dweck (2020) for a discussion), the core idea is a powerful one. One of the biggest learning barriers for me in the past has been what I’ll call the ‘finish line’ idea. It’s where I imagine myself reaching this hypothetical point where I’ll wake up one day and, aha, ‘know’ the subject. It’s a bad idea, of course, because very few subjects are small or well-defined enough that you could ever reach such a point.

It can also lead to a fixed mindset. If I see myself as not having reached the goal yet, maybe I’ll never get there; if I see myself as having reached the goal, maybe I don’t need to do anything else. This latter idea was one aspect of fixed mindsets that I hadn’t really thought about until exploring the topic this week. It had never occurred to me that having a fixed mindset can have negative effects even if you see yourself as competent in the domain in question.

Adopting a growth mindset is one way that I’m going to try to avoid the ‘finish line’ approach. I won’t ever know every single thing there is to know about development (I’m not sure that would even be possible in a human lifetime) and there’s not going to be a bell that dings to tell me I’m done. Instead of working towards some illusory end point, I’m going to focus on growing my skills and knowledge relative to what they were before. This might involve setting smaller goals and targets along the way, but I will try to avoid either getting discouraged because I’m not a master coder yet or getting complacent because I see myself as having done everything I need to do. There will always be more learning to do, and having a growth mindset means that that’s both possible and okay.
